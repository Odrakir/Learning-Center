Engineering Management — Glossary

The shared vocabulary for this workspace. Terms here are used consistently across every lesson.

Core rituals & roles

1:1 (one-on-one)
A recurring, private, individual meeting between a manager and a direct report. The report's meeting, not a status update. Origin of the modern practice: Andy Grove. — High Output Management
Direct report
A person who reports to you and whom you are responsible for managing and growing. Often shortened to "report."
IC (individual contributor)
An engineer who does the technical work directly, with no management reports. The role Ricardo came from.
Skip-level
A meeting between you and someone two levels down (or your manager and your reports). Used to sense-check team health beyond your direct view.

People & growth

Psychological safety
A shared belief that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking — you can admit mistakes, ask questions, and disagree without fear. The strongest predictor of effective teams. — Amy Edmondson; Google's Project Aristotle
Radical Candor
Giving feedback that combines Caring Personally with Challenging Directly. The opposite failure modes: Ruinous Empathy (care, no challenge) and Obnoxious Aggression (challenge, no care). — Kim Scott
SBI
A feedback structure: Situation → Behavior → Impact. Keeps feedback specific and non-judgmental.
Coaching (vs. telling)
Helping someone reach their own conclusion by asking questions, rather than handing them the answer. "Keep the thought bubble over their head."

Output & leverage

Managerial leverage
Output of a manager = output of their team + output of teams they influence. Your job is to maximise the team's output, not your own. — Andy Grove
Delegation
Handing real ownership of work (not just tasks) to a report. The main way a manager scales and a report grows. Transfer both the responsibility and the authority to decide — responsibility without authority is just disguised micromanagement.
Task-Relevant Maturity (TRM)
How ready a specific person is to own a specific task — a mix of skill, experience, and willingness to take responsibility. It is per-task, not a fixed rating of the person: the same engineer can be high-TRM on one task and low on another. Your monitoring depth should scale inversely with their TRM. — Andy Grove
Micromanagement
Controlling how work is done rather than owning the outcome. The usual cause is mismatching support to TRM (or never transferring decision authority), not malice.
Legibility
How visible and understandable the team's work is to people outside it. A great EM makes the team's progress and value legible to the org.
Outcome vs. output
Output = what was shipped. Outcome = the change in the world that resulted. Great EMs optimise for outcomes.
