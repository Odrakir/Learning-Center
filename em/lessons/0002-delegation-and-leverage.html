Delegation & Leverage

The hardest instinct to unlearn after being an IC — and the one that decides whether your team scales past you.
Why this, now? Coming from iOS, your reflex is to do the work. That reflex splits two ways on your team: you'll cling to the iOS work you're best at, and you may over-hand-off Backend/QA because you're less sure there. Both are delegation failures. This lesson gives you one dial — Task-Relevant Maturity — that tells you exactly how much to let go, in any discipline, even ones you've never practised. (See your mission.)

Leverage: the whole job in one line

Your output = the output of your team + the output of teams you influence.

That's Andy Grove's definition of managerial leverage. The work you do with your own hands no longer counts much. A single hour spent making five engineers better is worth more than an hour spent shipping one feature yourself. Delegation isn't offloading the work you dislike — it's the mechanism by which you create output at all, and the main way your people grow.

"If you're the smartest person in the room on every task, your team's ceiling is you."

The dial: Task-Relevant Maturity

The mistake is treating delegation as a switch — either you hand it over completely or you hover. Grove's fix is a dial. Every person has a Task-Relevant Maturity (TRM) for a specific task: their skill + experience + readiness to own it. Your monitoring depth should scale inversely with their TRM.

Their TRM for the taskWhat you giveHow you check in
Low — new to itClear outcome + the how, structured supportFrequently; review the work closely
Medium — done similarOutcome + guardrails, let them choose the howAgreed checkpoints / milestones
High — proven hereThe outcome and the why; full authorityLightly; they pull you in as needed

Three things make TRM click:

  • It's per-task, not per-person. A senior iOS engineer is high-TRM on a refactor, maybe low-TRM running their first incident. Re-read the dial for each task.
  • Delegate the outcome, not the steps. "The release needs to be reliable by Thursday" beats "run these five commands." Outcomes leave room for ownership; steps create dependence. (This is your cross-discipline unlock — you can own a backend outcome without knowing the backend steps.)
  • Transfer authority with the responsibility. If they have to ask you before every decision, you haven't delegated — you've created a queue at your desk.

And the cross-discipline reassurance: delegation calibrates to their TRM, not yours. You don't need to be able to write the Postgres migration to delegate it well — you need to know how proven they are at it and own the outcome and the checkpoints.
