The 1:1

The single highest-leverage habit you can build as a new manager — and your best tool for leading people whose work you can't read.
Why this, first? You came from iOS and now lead Backend and QA engineers too — crafts you've never practised. You can't lead them by reviewing their code. The 1:1 is how great managers stay connected, build trust, and grow people regardless of discipline. Master this one ritual and you have a foundation that works across your whole team. (See your mission.)

The idea, in 90 seconds

A 1:1 is a recurring, private meeting between you and one direct report. Andy Grove, who popularised it at Intel, framed it as a high-leverage act: a small time investment that raises the quality of a person's work for weeks.

The trap almost every new manager falls into: turning the 1:1 into a status update. Status belongs in standups, tickets, and chat. The 1:1 is for the things that don't surface anywhere else — blockers, growth, frustrations, ambitions, how they're really doing.

"It's their meeting, not yours. Aim for the report to do ~70% of the talking."

Four principles do most of the work:

  • Consistency & care. Same time, same place, weekly. Don't cancel — moving it tells them they don't matter.
  • They own the agenda. Come prepared with your topics, but lead with theirs. A shared doc both edit keeps history.
  • Coach, don't solve. When you want to jump in with the answer, ask another question instead. "Keep the thought bubble over their head."
  • Privacy & presence. A real private space, no laptops to distract, genuine listening. Use silence — the important stuff surfaces when the conversation wanders.

This is also your cross-discipline superpower: you don't need to out-code a backend engineer to ask "what's slowing you down?", "what would make this project great?", or "where do you want to grow?". Those questions work everywhere.

Check yourself

No looking back. Retrieving from memory is what makes it stick.
