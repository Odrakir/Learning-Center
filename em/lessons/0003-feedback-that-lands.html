Lesson 03 · Feedback · Engineering Management

Feedback That Lands

The skill that closes the loop on everything else — and the one most new managers either soften into mush or skip entirely.
Why this, now? Lesson 1 gave you the channel (the 1:1). Lesson 2 gave you the situations (you delegate, someone runs with it — well, or not). Feedback is the skill that turns those moments into growth. And it's your cross-discipline unlock: you can give a Backend or QA engineer sharp, useful feedback on behaviour and impact without ever being able to out-code them. You don't need to review the migration to tell them what missing the heads-up cost the team. (See your mission.)

The two axes: Radical Candor

Kim Scott's whole model is two independent things you do at once. Care Personally — you give a damn about the person, not just their output. Challenge Directly — you say the hard thing, clearly, to their face. Most people are good at one and drop the other under pressure. Plot them and you get four quadrants:

↑ Care Personally

Ruinous Empathy

Care, but won't challenge. "It was fine, don't worry." The most common failure for nice, new managers — it feels kind and quietly stalls the person.

Radical Candor ✓

Care and challenge. You say the hard thing because you're invested in them. The target.

Manipulative Insincerity

Neither. Backstabbing, faint praise, political silence. The worst quadrant.

Obnoxious Aggression

Challenge, but no care. Brutal honesty that lands as an attack. Better than silence, worse than candour.

Challenge Directly →

"Radical Candor is what happens when you put 'Care Personally' and 'Challenge Directly' together."

The trap to name for yourself: coming from IC, and new to the team, the gravitational pull is toward Ruinous Empathy — you want to be liked, you're still building trust, so you soften the criticism until it carries no information. That's not kindness. Withholding the thing that would help someone grow is a failure of care, not an expression of it.

The structure: SBI

Radical Candor is the stance. SBI is the sentence — a three-part structure (from the Center for Creative Leadership) that keeps feedback specific and non-defensive. It works identically for praise and criticism.

Situation
Anchor it to a specific time and place. "In yesterday's release sign-off…"
Behaviour
What they actually did — observable, not interpreted. "…you shipped without flagging the failing smoke test in the channel."
Impact
The concrete effect — on the work, the team, you. "…so QA found it in prod and we lost two hours rolling back, and I was caught off guard in standup."

Why SBI defuses defensiveness: it sticks to facts and consequences, not character. Compare "you're careless" (a verdict on who they are — instantly defensive) with "the smoke test was failing and it wasn't flagged" (a fact they can't argue with, and can fix). This is the cross-discipline key: you don't need backend expertise to observe a behaviour and name its impact. Behaviour and impact are discipline-agnostic.

Three rules that make feedback land regardless of the words:

  • Timely beats polished. Feedback decays fast. A rough, kind sentence within a day beats a perfect one in three weeks. Don't stockpile it for the review.
  • Criticism in private, praise in public. Criticism is for the 1:1. Praise — also SBI, also specific — is often best said where others hear it. "Good job" is forgettable; "the way you reproduced that flaky test with a minimal repro saved the team a day" is fuel.
  • Solicit it before you dish it. Ask for feedback on yourself first ("what could I be doing better?") and sit with the discomfort. It builds the trust that makes your criticism land — and models that feedback is safe here.
